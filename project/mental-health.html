01

Business Problem & Objective

Many organizations face challenges in understanding how mental health impacts employee productivity. This project aims to:

  • Assess the relationship between mental health risk levels, specific conditions, and employee productivity.
  • Evaluate the impact of lifestyle, social factors, and treatment-seeking on productivity among at-risk employees.
  • Analyze differences in productivity and mental health risk across gender and work environments.

Data Cleaning & Preparation

  • Removed employees with non-employed statuses (Student, Self-employed, Unemployed).
  • Rounded sleep_hours and productivity_score to whole numbers.
  • Converted Yes/No responses in mental_health_history and seeks_treatment to binary (1/0).
  • Checked for null, blank, or duplicate entries; none were found.
  • Ensured consistent formatting of categorical variables (gender, work_environment, mental_health_risk).
05

Data Modeling & DAX

  • Created calculated columns for treatment status and productivity status — segmenting medium/high-risk employees with productivity score (<70) into Affected, and Not Affected (≥70), while low-risk employees were classified as N/A and excluded from focused visuals.
  • Built dynamic measures for employee counts and productivity score statistics.
  • Implemented Pearson correlation manually in DAX (covariance ÷ standard deviations) to assess relationships between mental health factors, conditions and productivity.
  • Developed supporting dimension tables to structure correlation visuals.

08

Key Insights

33% of medium/high-risk employees scored below the productivity threshold (<70); 50% scored ≥70.
Sleep, physical activity days, and social support showed little to no impact on productivity among at-risk employees.
40% of at-risk employees sought treatment; no substantial difference in productivity scores was observed.
Higher depression scores correlated with lower productivity; anxiety and stress levels showed no meaningful association.
Productivity scores were similar across remote, hybrid, and on-site employees. On-site employees were overrepresented in risk categories.
Productivity scores were comparable across genders; gender differences in risk were minimal.
Employees with prior mental health conditions did not differ significantly in productivity or risk levels compared to those without.
09

Recommendations

  • Prioritize interventions targeting employees with high depression scores.
  • Implement proactive mental health monitoring for on-site employees.
  • Consider holistic support programs rather than relying solely on lifestyle interventions.
  • Encourage treatment-seeking without assuming immediate productivity gains.
  • Use this analysis as a baseline for longitudinal tracking to measure impact of mental health programs.
10

Conclusion

This analysis identified that depression is the primary mental health factor associated with productivity decline. Lifestyle factors and treatment-seeking behavior showed limited association. The insights enable data-driven decisions to target support for at-risk employees, particularly those with elevated depression scores and on-site work arrangements.

11

Limitations

  • Cross-sectional data; cannot infer causality.
  • Lack of time-sequenced data to track changes in productivity following lifestyle changes or treatment.
  • Dataset does not include additional factors like workload, managerial support, or personal stressors that may affect productivity.
