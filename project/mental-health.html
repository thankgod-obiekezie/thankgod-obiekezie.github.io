Preparation

Data cleaning & modelling

The raw dataset required several cleaning and engineering steps before analysis:

01 Removed non-employed statuses — Student, Self-employed, Unemployed — to focus on workplace-relevant records
02 Rounded sleep_hours and productivity_score to whole numbers for cleaner aggregation
03 Converted Yes/No fields (mental_health_history, seeks_treatment) to binary 1/0 flags
04 Verified zero null, blank, or duplicate entries across all columns
05 Standardised formatting across all categorical variables — gender, work_environment, mental_health_risk

In Power BI, calculated columns segmented medium/high-risk employees with productivity <70 as Affected vs ≥70 as Not Affected. Pearson correlation was implemented manually in DAX (covariance ÷ standard deviations) to assess factor relationships with productivity.

Findings

Key insights

33% of medium/high-risk employees scored below the productivity threshold (<70); 50% scored ≥70, suggesting risk level alone does not determine output.
Sleep hours, physical activity, and social support showed little to no association with productivity among at-risk employees — contrary to common assumptions.
40% of at-risk employees sought treatment, but no substantial productivity difference was observed between treatment-seekers and non-seekers.
Depression scores correlated most strongly with lower productivity. Anxiety and stress levels showed no meaningful association — pointing to depression as the primary clinical lever.
Productivity was similar across remote, hybrid, and on-site employees, though on-site workers were overrepresented in higher-risk categories.
Gender differences in risk levels and productivity were minimal, and prior mental health history did not significantly predict current risk or productivity scores.
Action

Recommendations

🎯
Prioritise interventions targeting employees with high depression scores specifically — not mental health risk broadly.
🏢
Implement proactive mental health monitoring for on-site employees, who are overrepresented in higher-risk categories.
🔄
Consider holistic support programmes beyond lifestyle interventions — sleep and exercise alone showed limited impact on productivity.
📈
Use this analysis as a baseline for longitudinal tracking — measure intervention impact over time rather than expecting immediate gains.
Summary

Conclusion

Depression emerged as the primary clinical factor associated with productivity decline — while lifestyle variables and treatment-seeking showed limited predictive power. This challenges blanket wellness programme assumptions and argues for more targeted, condition-specific interventions. The insights enable HR teams to allocate support resources where the data shows they matter most: at-risk employees with elevated depression scores and on-site work arrangements.

Caveats

Limitations

Cross-sectional data — associations identified here cannot be interpreted as causal relationships.
No time-sequenced data to track productivity changes following lifestyle shifts or treatment interventions.
Key confounders absent from the dataset: workload, managerial support, compensation, and personal stressors.
